Agricultural Predictions
How does maintaining optimal humidity levels, alongside other enviromental factors, influence the crop’s value!


Abstract

This study explores the complex interplay between soil moisture, nutrient availability (N, P, K)[1], and crop profitability in the Indian agricultural context. Further, emphasizes the combined effects of soil moisture and environmental factors on nutrient dynamics, providing valuable insights for optimizing irrigation practices and maximizing agricultural profit in India. While the research indicates dependencies between these variables, the findings suggest that pinpointing a universal optimal ratio for maximum yield and profit is inconclusive due to the complex influence of numerous external factors.

Introduction

Millions of Indians depend on agriculture[2]. Despite agriculture's vital role in the nation's economy, suboptimal crop choices limit the maximum yield and profits for specific soil conditions. This research aims to empower these farmers by finding optimal crops for each soil condition by analyzing past data and profit margins. Exploring the link between optimal soil moisture and NPK allows for improved irrigation practices, resulting in increased crop yields, enhanced food security, and a flourishing agricultural sector in India[3]. Data from ICFA IBER[4] and Kaggle[5] will be employed to achieve these goals. This prediction the stakeholder to find the most suitable crop for his particular location.

Exploring the connection between optimal soil moisture levels and the availability of crucial nutrients NPK[1]. Aiming to empower farmers to manage irrigation practices effectively by data-driven insights and a deeper understanding of soil science. Thereby increasing crop yields, improving food security, and improving agricultural economy for India.

The dataset "Crop Recommendation Dataset" was published by ICFA IBER of Food and Agriculture [4] by augmenting rainfall, climate, and fertilizer data available for India. Additionally, a database from Kaggle[5] is used to extrapolate data based on the price values of each crop and average NPK values to conclude how the nutrients and weather conditions might affect the financial profit from each crop. This allows the stakeholder to find the most suitable crops based on various parameters.

Literature Review

Exploring the critical roles of NPK and the influence of environmental factors on crop growth[6]. Emphasizing the complex interplay between these elements and their ultimate impact on crop yield. Micronutrients such as NPK play an essential role in the health and production of any crop.

Phosphorus (P): Plays a crucial role in flowering, seed production, photosynthesis, and overall plant growth[7]. Warmer temperatures favour P release, but excessively high temperatures can decrease soil moisture and hinder accessibility[8]. Moderate moisture is vital, as extreme rainfall can leach P, while dry conditions limit nutrient movement[9]

Potassium (K): While its impact varies, it significantly influences plant growth and yield. Similar to P, it thrives in moder-ate soil moisture and warmer temperatures[10], [11].

Nitrogen (N): Unlike P and K, low moisture can hinder nitrogen uptake by slowing down microbial activity that affects nitrogen mineralization. N transforms (mineralization, nitrification, immobilization) with different ideal temperatures; hence, there is no single ideal temperature [12].

While N differs in its response to moisture compared to P and K, all three nutrients are significantly affected by the interplay of moisture and other environmental factors.

Soil pH: Affects nutrient availability, not total NPK amount. NPK is the total quantity of nutrients in the soil, regardless of plant access[13]. Therefore, while the total NPK value might remain constant, the usable amount of these nutrients for plants can vary significantly depending on the soil's pH level[14].

Rainfall: Affects soil moisture, influencing nutrient uptake and potentially leading to drought stress (insufficient) or waterlogging (excessive)[3]

Humidity: : Doesn't change total NPK but can indirectly affect availability [15].High humidity: Boosts microbial activity, potentially releasing locked-up nutrients. And excessive humidity: Increases leaching, potentially removing nutrients.

Temperature: Directly influences plant growth and development, with deviations from the optimal range impacting pro-cesses like photosynthesis and pollination[12]

This intricate interaction between soil properties, water availability, and climate significantly impacts plant nutrient uptake and growth needs, ultimately affecting crop yield.

Hypothesis

As rainfall increases, soil moisture content will generally increase, leading to improved plant growth up to a certain point. However, excessive rainfall can leach away essential nutrients, potentially hindering plant growth if not replenished. Additionally, humidity levels may indirectly contribute to plant growth by reducing the evaporation rate from the soil, but its overall impact depends on factors like temperature

The data points concentrated towards the bottom of the K-P scatter plot indicate a positive skew in the distribution but with a lower bound for the combined K and P values. The positive skew reinforces the idea that most soil samples have lower combined K and P content than a potential maximum value. However, unlike a simple leftward shift, there is a minimum threshold for the combined K and P levels. This means that no samples fall below a certain level of these nutrients. Hence agricultural practices fertilizer a minimum amount of K and P ensuring a baseline level of these nutrients in the soil.

Conclusions

The findings revealed dependencies between rainfall and various plant growth factors, supporting the idea that rainfall plays a significant role in plant health. However, pinpointing a universally optimal rainfall level for maximizing yield and profit proved inconclusive. This outcome underscores the complex interplay between rainfall and numerous external factors.

As hypothesized, increasing rainfall generally improved plant growth by enhancing soil moisture content. However, excessive precipitation could have negative consequences, including nutrient leaching and increased humidity that could hinder plant development. These findings partially support the hypothesis, highlighting the intricate relationship between rainfall and plant growth. Future research efforts should explore these interactions in a controlled environment to isolate the specific effects of each variable and establish more precise recommendations for the stakeholders.
